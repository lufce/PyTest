Figure 1

smFRET Assay for Direct Visualization of HA Conformational Dynamics

(A) Viral particles containing a single HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 protomer, labeled at positions 17 and 127 in the HA2 domain, within an HA trimer were immobilized on a quartz microscope slide and imaged at room temperature with TIRF microscopy ( STAR Methods ).

(B) Molecular models of HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 predict that fluorophores placed at positions 17 and 127 in the HA2 domain will report on the transition from the pre-fusion to the coiled-coil conformation via changes in FRET efficiency (models were based on PDB: 2FK0 and 1HTM; STAR Methods ) ( Bullough et al., 1994a; Stevens et al., 2006 ).

See also Figure S4 .

Figure 2

Direct Visualization of pH-Triggered Conformational Changes in HA

(A) Left: Contour plots constructed from the compilation of the population of smFRET trajectories. The contour plots were summed over time, generating FRET histograms, which are shown with three Gaussian distributions overlaid (red) with means ± SD of 0.20 ± 0.08, 0.53 ± 0.09, and 0.95 ± 0.06 that represent the states identified through HMM analysis (black). The number of smFRET traces (n) compiled into each histogram is indicated. Right: TDPs displaying the distributions of initial and final FRET values for every observed transition in FRET. The number of observed transitions (n) and the total transitions per second (t/s) are indicated.

(B–E) The same data acquired at the indicated pH.

See also Figure S3 and Table S1 .

Figure 3

Pre-steady-state smFRET Imaging Indicates an Intermediate Conformation Adopted during Triggering of HA by Acidic pH

(A) Representative fluorescence (donor, green; acceptor, red) and FRET trajectories (blue) obtained from a single HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 protomer within an HA trimer on the surface of a viral particle. At each time point, FRET efficiency was calculated as the ratio of acceptor fluorescence intensity to total fluorescence intensity. Overlaid on the FRET trajectory in red is an idealized trace generated through HMM analysis.

(B) Contour plot of smFRET trajectories synchronized to the transition out of high FRET. Transition through the intermediate-FRET state is observed in route to low FRET.

(C) As in Figure 2 , the TDP displays the distribution in initial and final FRET values for every transition observed. The TDP indicates that trajectories predominantly transition from high to intermediate FRET, and from intermediate to low FRET. A minority of transitions occurs directly from high to low FRET with no observation of intermediate FRET. This can be explained by the instability of the intermediate-FRET state and the limited time resolution of our imaging.

Figure 4

Restoration of the Pre-fusion Conformation after Return to Neutral pH

(A–L) FRET contour plot, histogram, and TDP acquired immediately after exposure of HA to (A) pH 5.6 (repeated from Figure 2 ), (B) pH 5.3, or (C) pH 5.2. The same data were acquired after returning to pH 7.5 following (D–F) 5 min, (G–I) 15 min, or (J–L) 30 min. Data are displayed as in Figure 2 .

See also Table S1 .

Figure 5

Interaction with a Sialic-Acid-Containing Target Membrane Regulates HA Conformational Dynamics

(A) Schematic of the smFRET-imaging assay in the presence of liposomes with GD1a, which contains sialic acid ( STAR Methods ).

(B) Representative fluorescence and FRET traces at pH 6.1 displayed as in Figure 2 .

(C) FRET contour plots (left), histograms (middle), and TDPs (right) displayed as in Figure 2 .

(D and E) Quantification of the occupancies in the (D) high-FRET pre-fusion and (E) low-FRET conformations in the absence (blue) and presence (red) of liposomes, which was determined through HMM analysis.

Figure 6

A Sialic-Acid-Containing Target Membrane Promotes Transition of HA to the Irreversible Coiled-Coil Conformation

(A) FRET contour plot, histograms, and TDPs acquired in the presence of GD1a-containing liposome immediately after exposure to pH 5.6.

(B and C) The same data were acquired after return to pH 7.5 following (B) 5 min or (C) 30 min. Data are displayed as in Figure 2 .

Figure 7

Kinetic Model of HA2 Conformational Dynamics

(A) smFRET analysis suggests a sequence of conformational changes in HA during conversion from the pre-fusion to the coiled-coil conformation. The HA2 domain reversibly interconverts between three conformations (pre-fusion, intermediate state I, and intermediate state II). Low pH stalls HA2 in intermediate state II in the absence of a receptor-containing membrane, from which it can return to the pre-fusion state by reneutralizing the pH.

(B) Interaction with the receptor-containing target membrane promotes formation of the coiled-coil conformation.

(C–F) The rate constants k1, k-1, k2, are k-2 were determined by exponential fitting of the dwell-time histograms obtained through HMM analysis ( STAR Methods ). Rates of transition between the observed states are shown as a function of pH in the (C and D) absence or (E and F) presence of a receptor-containing target membrane. Error bars represent the 95% confidence intervals obtained during fitting of the dwell time histograms. The plotted lines represent linear fits.

See also Table S3 .

TCO∗ ncAA Incorporation and Site-Specific Fluorescent Labeling of HA, Related to Figure 1

(A) Schematic of engineered influenza HA∗, and sequence alignment of wild-type VN04 HA and HA∗. The locations of TCO∗ residues at positions 17 and 127 are shown as asterisks.

(B) Schematic outlining the steps involved in amber stop codon suppression and fluorescent labeling for VN04 HA pseudotyped on the HIV-1 core. See STAR Methods for details.

(C) Western blot indicating the presence of wild-type VN04 HA0 and HA on the HIV-1 core. VN04 HA is efficiently cleaved by cellular furin. HA0, formed through mutation of the polybasic furin cleavage site, shows no cleavage. Lysate from cells transfected with VN04 HA0 and HA017TAG/127TAG plasmid. Generation of full length HA0∗ is dependent on the presence of NESPylRSAF/tRNAPyl and TCO∗ ncAA. Likewise, in viral supernatant, full-length VN04 HA0∗ is dependent on the presence of NESPylRSAF/tRNAPyl and TCO∗ ncAA. The antibody targeting HA1 was purchased from Thermo Fisher.

(D) Pseudotyped virions containing wild-type HA or HA∗ were subjected to the fluorescent labeling reaction, surface immobilized, and imaged using TIRF microscopy as described in STAR Methods . Surface-localized fluorescence was only observed for HA∗ indicating specific attachment of the fluorophore. Shown here are representative fields of view.

Figure S2

HA∗ and HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 Are Functional in Membrane Fusion and Maintain Native Reactivity with Antibodies, Related to Figures 1 and 2

(A) The fusogenicity of pseudotyped virions containing HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 was evaluated using a Blam-based virus entry assay ( Cavrois et al., 2002 ). As compared to virions with wild-type VN04 HA, virions containing HA∗ demonstrated approximately 80% fusogenicity with A549 target cells, and HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 demonstrated approximately 76% fusogenicity. KB2 and CR9114 (1.5 μM) antibodies were incubated with the virions at 37°C for 30 minutes before passage to A549 target cells. VSV-G was used as a positive control for the virus-cell fusion assay.

(B) A bulk fluorescence-based lipid mixing assay indicated dequenching of the lipophilic fluorophore (DiO) due to fusion of HA-pseudotyped virions to liposomes containing GD1a ( Wessels et al., 2007 ). The kinetics of fusion for wild-type HA and HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 were equivalent across a range of pHs. This indicates no loss of functionality or change in pH dependence for the modified HA.

(C) ELISA assays performed with four HA stalk-binding antibodies (CR9114, FI6, KB2, and 6F12) showing equivalent binding to pseudovirions with HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 and wild-type HA.

Fluorescence and FRET Traces Showing HA2 Conformational Sampling, Related to Figure 2

(A) Representative fluorescence (donor, green; acceptor, red) and FRET trajectories (blue) obtained from single HA∗-Cy3/Cy5 protomers within HA trimers on the surface of viral particles at pH 7. At each time point FRET efficiency was calculated as the ratio of acceptor fluorescence intensity to total fluorescence intensity. Overlaid on the FRET trajectory in red is an idealized trace generated through HMM analysis.

(B–E) The same data acquired at the indicated pH.

Figure S4

MD Simulations of Fluorophore Dynamics in the Pre-fusion and Post-fusion Coiled-Coil Conformations, Related to Figure 1

The mobility of the fluorophores on HA in the (blue) pre-fusion and (red) post-fusion coiled-coil conformations were evaluated by MD simulation ( STAR Methods ).

(A) The average inter-fluorophore distances over the 50 ns simulations were 39 ± 3 Å and 105 ± 2 Å, for pre-fusion and post-fusion coiled-coil conformations, respectively. These distances are consistent with the observed 0.95-FRET state for the pre-fusion conformation, and the 0.20-FRET state for the post-fusion conformation.

(B) Snap-shots of the structural models are shown with the centers of mass of the fluorophores from each frame in the simulation indicated with green (Cy3) and red (Cy5) spheres.

Figure S5

Mutagenesis of HA and Stalk-Targeting Antibodies Confirm the Identification of FRET States, Related to Figures 2 and 3

The furin cleavage site was mutated (RKKR/QKQQ) to abrogate proteolytic processing.

(A–C) Top: Population FRET contour plot and histograms with Gaussian fits overlaid acquired from HA0 at (A) pH 7. Bottom: TDPs indicating relative frequencies of the observed transitions. Similarly, smFRET traces, contour plot, histogram and TDP plot for data acquired with HA0 at (B) pH 6.1 and (C) pH 5.3. HA0 maintains significant conformational dynamics across all pHs considered.

(D and E) The G4E mutation in the fusion peptide of HA2 prevents stable formation of the high-FRET pre-fusion conformation. (top) FRET contour plot and histogram acquired from G4E HA at (D) pH 7 and (E) pH 5.6. (bottom) TDPs indicating relative frequencies of the observed transitions. The G4E HA undergoes significant fluctuations between the low-FRET state and the intermediate-FRET state, but never stably adopts the high-FRET pre-fusion state due to destabilization of the fusion peptide in the hydrophobic pocket.

(F) HA stalk-targeting neutralizing antibodies prevent formation of stable low FRET. (top) FRET contour plot, histogram and TDPs (bottom) acquired for HA in the presence of stalk-targeting neutralizing antibody CR9114 at pH 6.1.

(G) The same data acquired in the presence of antibody KB2 at pH 6.1.

In all panels, data are displayed as in Figure 2 .

Figure S6

Role of Soluble Receptor Analog LSTa and Target Membrane on HA Conformational Dynamics, Related to Figure 5

(A–F) FRET contour plot and histogram displayed as in Figure 2 in the presence of the soluble LSTa oligosaccharide. Data were acquired at the indicated pH.

(G–L) The same data acquired in the presence of biotinylated liposomes lacking sialic acid, which were bound to the virions containing a biotin-lipid in the viral membrane by way of a streptavidin bridge.

Figure S7

Interaction with the Target Membrane Promotes Transition to the Irreversible Coiled-Coil Conformation, Related to Figure 6

(A) Top: FRET contour plot, histograms and TDPs acquired in the presence of soluble LSTa immediately after exposure to pH 5.6. The same data were acquired after return to pH 7.5 following (middle) 5 min, or (bottom) 30 min.

(B) The same data acquired in the presence of a target membrane lacking sialic acid. Data are displayed as in Figure 2 .

Highlights

  • •Influenza HA spontaneously samples conformations related to membrane fusion
  • •Interaction with the receptor allosterically induces fusion peptide movement
  • •pH, receptor binding, and interaction with a target membrane regulate HA dynamics
  • •HA can return to the pre-fusion conformation after transient exposure to acidic pH

Summary

Influenza hemagglutinin (HA) is the canonical type I viral envelope glycoprotein and provides a template for the membrane-fusion mechanisms of numerous viruses. The current model of HA-mediated membrane fusion describes a static “spring-loaded” fusion domain (HA2) at neutral pH. Acidic pH triggers a singular irreversible conformational rearrangement in HA2 that fuses viral and cellular membranes. Here, using single-molecule Förster resonance energy transfer (smFRET)-imaging, we directly visualized pH-triggered conformational changes of HA trimers on the viral surface. Our analyses reveal reversible exchange between the pre-fusion and two intermediate conformations of HA2. Acidification of pH and receptor binding shifts the dynamic equilibrium of HA2 in favor of forward progression along the membrane-fusion reaction coordinate. Interaction with the target membrane promotes irreversible transition of HA2 to the post-fusion state. The reversibility of HA2 conformation may protect against transition to the post-fusion state prior to arrival at the target membrane.

Keywords:

virus entry, membrane fusion, protein dynamics, smFRET, single-molecule fluorescence
